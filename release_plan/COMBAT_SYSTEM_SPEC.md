# Divine Fantasy - Combat System Spec v1

## Purpose

This document defines what combat should be for the Driftwatch alpha/demo and how it should grow into the larger game later.

It exists separately from the route roadmap because combat is not just:

- damage formulas
- enemy stats
- weapons

It is a full gameplay pillar involving:

- brawls
- street violence
- beast danger
- combat skill growth
- agility and movement training
- coercion and intimidation overlap
- equipment progression
- companion combat support
- a future bridge into party management and larger-scale force

The goal is to make combat fit Luke, fit the 7-day demo, and leave room for the bigger late-game power fantasy.

## Core Combat Direction

The alpha combat route should **not** be a "beat the week by being a badass" route.

Luke is weak, underfed, under-equipped, and socially low-status at the start.

That means combat in the alpha should feel like:

- scrappy
- risky
- personal
- often ugly
- sometimes humiliating
- sometimes empowering
- never cleanly dominant

The player fantasy should be:

- "I am still weak, but I am less helpless than I look."

This makes combat a route of:

- rough survival
- nerve
- desperation
- selective aggression
- surviving ugly situations better than the other starts

## Combat Route Role In The Alpha

Combat should be a **secondary but real** route in Story Mode.

It should be stronger as:

- self-defense
- escalation
- intimidation support
- rough opportunity access
- survival training

It should be weaker as:

- main debt-solving route
- stable economy route
- dominant story-winning path

This means:

- combat helps Luke survive certain scenes
- combat helps Luke take rough shortcuts
- combat helps Luke train into someone harder
- combat does not replace skilling or social as the main week structure

## Combat Route Role In Sandbox / Full Game

Combat should become more important outside the tight 7-day story pressure.

That means combat is likely:

- a modest support route in Story Mode
- a much bigger progression route in Sandbox and the full game

This is where the player can eventually lean into:

- repeatable fights
- dangerous jobs
- underground arenas
- hunting
- companions
- leadership
- larger force and command fantasy later

So the alpha should support the beginning of that path, not its full destination.

## Core Combat Loop

The alpha combat loop should be:

1. find or trigger a fight opportunity
2. decide whether to escalate, fight, flee, or pressure
3. risk HP, time, food, and gear progression
4. gain XP, money, fear, access, or route identity
5. recover and decide whether to keep pushing

This means combat is built from three pillars:

### 1. Brawls

Low-lethality human fights that teach combat and let weak Luke win believable victories.

### 2. Street Violence

Real human danger with knives, clubs, ambushes, or organized threats.

### 3. Beast Danger

Wild fights that remind the player nature is brutal and early Luke is not ready for everything.

## Fight Type 1: Brawls

Brawls should be the main early combat training lane.

Examples:

- tavern fights
- drunken disputes
- pit fights
- clandestine arena fights
- gambling-backed hand-to-hand bouts

### Why Brawls Matter

Brawls solve a major tone problem:

- Luke can realistically beat weak or drunk humans
- the player can train combat without becoming implausibly strong
- combat can have a repeatable early loop without making wolves farmable

### Brawl Rules

Brawls should generally mean:

- fists only
- no lethal intent by default
- lower damage than knife fights
- lower stakes than beast fights
- smaller loot and money outcomes

### Brawl Rewards

Brawls should reward:

- Attack XP
- Agility XP in some cases
- small money through bets or winnings, usually in copper at this stage
- local fear/reputation in rough spaces later if desired

### Brawl Content Ideas

For the alpha, brawls could include:

- Ben drunk in the tavern
- a new tavern fighter NPC
- a hidden slum arena unlocked through someone like Shaky Jace
- low-stakes copper betting on fights
- harder brawlers as a progression ladder

This is likely the most important combat addition for making the route feel believable.

## Fight Type 2: Street Violence

Street violence is where combat becomes truly dangerous.

Examples:

- thugs
- muggers
- smugglers
- rough debt confrontations
- slum ambushes
- knife fights

### Purpose

These fights should define the combat route as:

- threatening
- unstable
- harsher than brawls
- more story-relevant than pit fighting

### Rules

Street fights should feel:

- riskier than brawls
- more gear-sensitive
- more coercion-adjacent
- more likely to injure or kill a weak Luke

### Rewards

Street violence should reward:

- faster progress in select scenes
- rough money
- fear-based leverage
- survival identity

But not:

- safe grinding
- easy route dominance

## Fight Type 3: Beast Danger

Beast fights should be their own category, not just "another enemy type."

Examples:

- wolves

### Purpose

These fights exist to show:

- nature does not care about Luke
- beasts are not drunks or weak debtors
- gear and preparation matter

### Balance Direction

Early-game target feeling:

- 1 wolf with a dagger might be survivable
- 2 wolves should be near-certain death for weak unarmored Luke
- 3-4 wolves should be obvious "do not do this" territory unless prepared

This is important because it preserves the harsh survival tone.

Wolves should not become the main early combat training path.

They should be dangerous, memorable, and gear-gated.

## Combat Progression Philosophy

Combat progression should not mean:

- Luke becomes a dominant killer during the demo

It should mean:

- Luke becomes less helpless
- Luke becomes better at surviving fights
- Luke becomes more credible in violent spaces
- Luke becomes more dangerous to weak humans

The route arc for the 7-day demo should feel like:

### Day 1-2

- Luke is weak
- fists and a dagger matter, but barely
- brawls are believable
- wolves are terrifying
- fleeing is normal

### Day 3-4

- first armor or gear upgrades matter a lot
- weak human opponents become manageable
- rough opportunities become more tempting

### Day 5-6

- Luke is still not a warrior
- but he is clearly harder than before
- combat can support a few meaningful shortcuts

### Day 7

- the player feels like Luke has become dangerous in a small, local, ugly way
- not powerful in a heroic sense

## Core Combat Stats

For the alpha, combat should primarily care about:

- `Attack`
- `Defence`
- `Agility`
- `Coercion`

### Attack

Represents:

- direct fighting competence
- damage output
- confidence in rough fights

Used for:

- brawls
- weapon attacks
- offensive combat progression

### Defence

Represents:

- toughness
- ability to absorb punishment
- gear-supported survivability

Used for:

- surviving knife fights
- surviving beasts
- making armor matter

### Agility

Represents:

- initiative
- movement
- escape
- speed and responsiveness

Used for:

- turn order
- fleeing
- some future dirty-fighting or movement actions

### Coercion

Represents:

- intimidation
- threat
- pressure through presence or violence

Used for:

- fear-based dialogue outcomes
- ambush avoidance or intimidation outcomes
- skipping some weak-human fights when the player looks dangerous enough

Coercion should remain a bridge between social and combat, not a pure combat stat.

## Initiative And Dexterity / Agility

Dexterity/Agility should matter more honestly than it does now.

Current player-priority initiative is useful for reducing frustration, but it weakens the stat.

The intended direction should be:

- if an enemy is faster, they should often act first
- initiative should feel more real outside tutorial protection

This matters because agility should not be a fake stat.

It should change how fights feel.

## Agility Training Outside Combat

Agility should not only level from fleeing or combat side effects.

The game should support dedicated agility growth routes such as:

- rooftop running
- parkour-style traversal
- courier work
- messenger jobs
- movement-based errands

This is important because it makes agility a world stat, not just a combat stat.

It also gives the combat route and broader sandbox progression more texture.

## Equipment Philosophy

Luke is weak, so gear must matter a lot.

Equipment should be the main thing that changes combat viability early.

### Weapon Role

Weapons should feel different by enemy type.

Example:

- fists are for brawls
- a dagger helps a lot against humans
- a dagger does not make Luke suddenly good against wolves
- blunt weapons belong to real street fights, not standard brawls

### Armor Role

Armor should be one of the biggest early combat upgrades in the game.

That means an early armor ladder should be visible and meaningful.

Suggested early progression:

- `Rags`
- `Padded Shirt`
- `Leather Vest`
- `Patched Leather`
- `Rough Hunter Gear`

Armor should sharply affect:

- survivability against humans
- survivability against beasts
- intimidation potential in social/event resolution

## Damage Types And Armor Classes

The current live combat implementation now uses a simple typed-damage layer.

### Live Damage Types

- `Blunt`
- `Pierce`
- `Slash`

### Live First-Pass Logic

- `Blunt` is the impact damage type and should remain most relevant for fists and clubs
- `Pierce` gets through protection better than fists
- `Slash` is the current beast/blade default and works best into soft or lightly protected targets

### Live Armor Classes

- `None`
- `Light`
- `Heavy`

Current practical read:

- no armor = Luke gets mauled and beaten up fast
- light armor = meaningfully better survival
- heavy armor = strongest protection, especially against fists

This is intentionally a simple alpha layer, not a full resistance matrix yet.

## Intimidation

Intimidation should exist, but it does not necessarily need to be a direct combat button.

The better direction is:

- intimidation as dialogue check
- intimidation as event choice
- intimidation as pre-combat or ambush resolution
- intimidation resolved through coercion plus intimidation/presentation value, modified by clothes, armor, and visible weapons

### Why

This is cleaner than adding a generic combat button that works everywhere.

It also fits the social presentation system already being designed.

### Examples

- a weak drunk backs down because Luke has a knife and rough gear
- a slum ambush can be avoided if Luke looks dangerous enough
- a cowardly debtor gives in if coercion and presentation are high enough
- wolves do not care how scary Luke looks

This makes intimidation:

- powerful in the right spaces
- useless in the wrong spaces

Which is exactly what it should be.

## Combat Actions For Alpha

The alpha combat UI should remain simple.

Current base:

- `Attack`
- `Flee`

Possible expansion should be small and readable.

Recommended first pass:

- keep `Attack`
- keep `Flee`
- consider one or two rough style actions later if needed

Possible additions:

- `Reckless Attack`
- `Dirty Trick`
- `Guard Break`

### Why not add too much

The route is still about weak Luke surviving ugly situations.

Too many abilities too early could overcomplicate the alpha and break tone.

If additional actions are added, they should feel:

- rough
- practical
- low-fantasy
- street-level

## Combat Techniques / Learnable Moves

A small move/technique system could be a very strong future layer.

This would work better than a generic list of RPG skills.

### Direction

Luke could eventually learn a small number of combat techniques from:

- trainers
- pit fighters
- soldiers
- hunters
- rough life experience

These could be slotted as a small equipped move set.

Examples:

- `Wild Swing`
- `Quick Jab`
- `Low Kick`
- `Dirty Strike`
- `Slip Away`
- `Guard Break`

### Alpha Recommendation

Do not fully build this yet unless scope allows.

But the combat design should leave room for it, because it is a better long-term progression model than pure stat inflation.

## Companion Role

Companions should matter in combat even in the alpha.

For the alpha:

- companions are active party members in small fights
- they help Luke survive situations he should not handle alone
- they hint at the later party-management fantasy

Companions should not yet become a full formation or army system.

But combat should already distinguish between:

- Luke
- active companions
- companions not currently traveling with him later

That matters for the long-term game shape.

## Finn Week Role

Combat should support the Finn week, but not dominate it.

That means combat in Story Mode should mostly appear as:

- tavern brawls
- rough escalation
- coercive pressure
- dangerous errands
- self-defense
- final confrontations

It should not become:

- the main debt economy
- the clean fastest route through every scene

### Good Finn-Week Combat Uses

- brawling Ben in the tavern when he is drunk
- roughing up a weak contact
- surviving smuggler violence
- escalating a scene when talk collapses
- Finn confrontation as a culmination of hardening over the week

## Existing In-Game Foundation

The current game already supports this direction more than it may seem.

Important existing supports:

- turn-based combat engine
- party combat support
- wolves as dangerous wilderness fights
- smugglers as small-group human threat
- Finn confrontation
- coercive dialogue branches
- attack/defence/agility XP hooks
- typed damage (`blunt`, `pierce`, `slash`)
- practical armor classes (`none`, `light`, `heavy`)
- first real brawl implementation
- combat loot screen and loot roll support

Important current gaps:

- only one real authored brawl exists so far
- no dedicated pit/arena content yet
- initiative is still too player-favored
- street-violence content is still very thin
- intimidation exists in a first authored branch, but is not broadly integrated yet
- equipment progression is not yet clearly framed around combat survival
- no repeatable slum arena loop yet
- no thug/knife-thug ambush events yet

## Alpha Build Priorities

The combat system should be built in this order:

1. define combat as scrappy survival, not dominance
2. add brawl content as the main early combat training lane
3. rebalance wolves and other beasts to be genuinely dangerous
4. expand beyond the first Ben brawl into a real early brawl lane
5. make agility/initiative more meaningful
6. add or tune early armor progression
7. improve dialogue/event intimidation using coercion + presentation
8. expand combat opportunities in sandbox/full-game directions later

## Recommended First Content Additions

The most valuable first additions would be:

- a tavern fighter NPC or shady unlock NPC like Shaky Jace
- one repeatable or semi-repeatable slum brawl loop
- one hidden slum arena using copper-scale payouts
- low-stakes betting for those fights
- one first real street-violence encounter using thug / knife thug content
- early armor pieces that noticeably matter
- one blunt weapon for street fighting
- movement/agility training activity or job

## Long-Term Growth Vision

Combat should grow in three stages:

### Early Game

- fists
- daggers
- tavern fights
- wolves
- smugglers
- survival scraps

### Mid Game

- better gear
- stronger companions
- learnable moves
- hunting, pit fighting, dangerous work
- clearer role differentiation in party members

### Late Game

- companion assignments
- businesses and holdings tied to force
- leadership and command
- followers, squads, or militia
- larger-scale violence managed through a different or expanded layer later

The important thing is:

- early combat should prepare for this vision
- but it should not try to simulate it yet

## What Should Not Be Built Yet

To protect scope, the alpha should not try to build:

- full army combat
- large formation warfare
- too many special combat buttons
- deep technique trees
- huge enemy variety for its own sake

The alpha only needs enough combat structure to prove that:

- brawls work
- danger matters
- gear matters
- Luke can grow from helpless to harder

## Success Standard

The combat system is successful for the alpha if:

- brawls give Luke a believable early combat lane
- wolves and beasts remain frightening
- human violence feels dangerous but manageable with growth
- gear noticeably changes survivability
- agility matters both in and out of combat
- intimidation works through coercion and presentation in authored cases
- combat feels stronger in sandbox/full-game potential without hijacking Story Mode

## Open Design Questions

These still need final decisions before implementation:

1. whether brawls should use the same combat screen with special rules or a slightly different ruleset
2. how betting should work in pit fights or tavern brawls
3. whether agility activities should be jobs, map actions, or minigame-like interactions
4. which specific Finn-week scenes should support brawling or violent escalation
5. whether learnable techniques should appear in the alpha at all or wait until later
